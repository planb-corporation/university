# Information Perception and Environmentally Conscious Behavior: An Evaluation Using Conjoint Analysis

**Master's Thesis** — Graduate School of Economics, Kyoto University (January 2007)

**Author:** Satoshi Baba

## Abstract

This thesis investigates how information provision through various media channels affects consumer preferences for environmentally friendly products. Using choice-based conjoint analysis, the study estimates consumers' willingness to pay (WTP) for product attributes such as origin, quality assurance labels, and freshness of vegetables (spinach), before and after a structured information intervention on food safety and environmental issues.

A field experiment was conducted with 305 subjects at Co-op Kobe stores in 2006. Subjects were divided into a treatment group (receiving periodic print-based environmental information) and a control group. The study employed both Conditional Logit (CL) and Nested Logit (NL) models for estimation.

### Key Findings

- **Print media information paradoxically decreased preferences for quality labels** — subjects who received structured information became more skeptical of current labeling systems, suggesting that reliable information can highlight shortcomings in existing certification frameworks.
- **Word-of-mouth was the most influential media channel** — it significantly increased preferences for quality labels, offsetting the negative effects of print information and explaining why the overall sample showed little net change.
- **Media exposure frequency did not directly increase environmentally conscious preferences** — while frequent media consumers had higher baseline preferences, the information itself did not produce significant changes during the study period.
- **Effective information provision requires consumer participation** — the findings suggest that trust formation through communication and involvement is more important than one-directional information delivery.

## Context

This research was conducted at a time when consumer trust in food labeling had been severely undermined by a series of fraudulent labeling incidents in Japan. The study's central question — how to build genuine consumer trust in environmental and quality information — remains highly relevant today, as issues of greenwashing, information asymmetry, and credence attributes continue to challenge sustainable consumption globally.
